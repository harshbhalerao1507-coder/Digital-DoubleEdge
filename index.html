Our Project Mandate

Our Mission

To quantitatively and qualitatively analyze mobile phone usage patterns among engineering students, identifying the tangible impacts on academic performance and mental well-being, and to propose actionable strategies for fostering a healthier digital culture on campus.

Core Objectives

  • Quantify Usage: Measure average daily screen time and identify the most-used application categories.
  • Academic Impact: Correlate usage patterns with self-reported levels of focus and academic distraction.
  • Wellness Insights: Uncover links between screen time, sleep quality, and feelings of anxiety (FOMO).
  • Promote Balance: Develop and share practical digital wellness strategies based on our findings.

The Two Sides of the Screen

The Upside: A Tool for Progress

  • Instant Access to Information: Research, online courses, and academic resources at your fingertips.
  • Enhanced Collaboration: Seamless communication with peers and faculty for group projects and discussions.
  • Skill Development: Apps for coding, language learning, and professional networking.
  • Safety & Navigation: GPS, emergency services, and staying connected with family.

The Downside: A Source of Distraction

  • Academic Disruption: Constant notifications and the lure of social media affecting focus in lectures.
  • Mental Health Impact: Cyberbullying, social comparison, and anxiety from 'Fear of Missing Out' (FOMO).
  • Sleep Deprivation: Blue light from screens disrupting natural sleep cycles.
  • Privacy Risks: Oversharing personal data and vulnerability to scams.

Recommendations: A Path to Digital Balance

Our findings highlight a clear need for mindful usage. The goal isn't phone elimination, but intentional integration. Here are data-driven strategies.

Set 'No-Phone' Zones

Designate times or places (like the dinner table or 1 hour before bed) as screen-free to improve sleep and social connection.

Curate Your Notifications

Turn off non-essential notifications. Our data shows a direct link between notification frequency and classroom distraction.

Use Digital Wellbeing Tools

Leverage built-in apps to monitor screen time. Aim to reduce time in non-productive apps identified in your weekly report.

Practice Mindful Scrolling

Before unlocking your phone, ask "Why?". Use your device with intent, not out of habit, to combat passive, time-consuming scrolling.

Replace with Offline Hobbies

Find something you enjoy that doesn’t involve a screen — like sketching, playing an instrument, cycling, or even cooking. The key is to make your hands busy enough that scrolling feels inconvenient.

Schedule Tech-Free Hours

Set specific hours daily — maybe right after waking up and an hour before bed — where you avoid all screens. Replace it with journaling, stretching, or reading a physical book.

Use the “Gray-Scale” Trick

Turn your phone’s display to grayscale mode. It removes color stimulation, making social media and games visually dull. Most people subconsciously reduce screen time because the phone stops feeling “fun.”

Uninstall or Limit Addictive Apps

Identify the top 2–3 apps eating your time (like Instagram or YouTube Shorts). Either uninstall them temporarily, or set app timers through your phone settings. You’ll be surprised how much calmer your day feels
